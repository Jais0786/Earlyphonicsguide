Why Sound Blending Is Hard for Many Preschool Readers

Many children learn letter sounds but struggle when it is time to combine those sounds into words. This skill is called blending. Instead of recognizing each sound separately, the brain must connect them smoothly to form a word.

For example, a child may know the sounds for c, a, and t, but blending requires them to hear the sounds together as cat.

Blending develops through listening practice, repetition, and exposure to simple sound patterns. When children are asked to read words before blending is comfortable, frustration often appears.

Signs a Child Is Still Learning Blending

  • They say sounds individually but cannot connect them
  • They guess words instead of sounding them out
  • Reading practice causes frustration
  • They skip sounds in simple words

These signs are common during early reading development and often improve when blending is practiced slowly and consistently.
